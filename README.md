The Neutrophil-to-Lymphocyte Ratio (NLR) is one of the most useful and underused numbers
  on a standard CBC. It costs nothing extra — it's already in your bloodwork — but most
  people have never heard of it.

  **What is NLR?**
  NLR = absolute neutrophil count ÷ absolute lymphocyte count

  Neutrophils = your innate "fire alarm" immune response
  Lymphocytes = your adaptive "precision defense" immune response

  When neutrophils chronically dominate, it's a marker of smoldering systemic inflammation
  — what researchers call "inflammaging." It's independently associated with accelerated
  biological aging and, in meta-analyses of 500k+ subjects, with all-cause mortality even
  after adjusting for age.

  **NHANES reference ranges (95,000 US adults, CDC data):**
  - < 1.5 → Unusually low (lymphocyte dominant, worth flagging)
  - 1.5–2.5 → Optimal
  - 2.5–4.0 → Mildly elevated
  - > 4.0 → High (independently associated with mortality risk in large studies)

  **To calculate yours:**
  Find "neutrophils absolute" and "lymphocytes absolute" on your CBC differential (the
  numbers, not the percentages). Divide neutrophils by lymphocytes.

  **Caveats I always include:**
  - Acute infection, recent intense exercise, or corticosteroids can transiently spike NLR
    by 1–2 points
  - A single value is context; a trend over 3–6 months is signal
  - Not a diagnostic — talk to your doctor if consistently > 4.0
